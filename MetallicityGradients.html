Metallicity Gradients: A deep dive

Alex M. Garcia

Mass Metallicity Relation

The metal content of galaxies has been increasingly appreciated as a constraint on galaxy evolution models. Metals are formed within stars, while on the main sequence or during the later stages of their evolutionary cycles, as such, the overall metal abundance of a galaxy is entirely determined by the star formation history of that galaxy. The metals from within the stars can be ejected back into the interstellar medium (ISM) through stellar (AGB) winds and supernovae explosions (SNe). Once back in the ISM, the metals mix with the ISM and are dragged along in galactic winds or mix via turbulence.

Figure 1: MZR from Tremonti et al. 2004

The link between galaxy the physical processes within a galaxy and its metal content gives rise to a key galaxy scaling relation: the mass metallicity relation (MZR; see Figure 1). This very tight relation describes a correlation of increasing metal content with increasing galaxy mass (see, e.g., Tremonti et al. 2004, Lee et al. 2006) Notably, this trend is not perfectly linear: there is a turn-over at the highest masses. Briefly, the different slope of the relation at these highest masses is indicative of the efficacy of the physical processes (e.g., winds) at the different masses.

Metallicity of individual galaxies

While the MZR is an incredibly powerful tool for studying and understanding galaxy populations, individual systems are a little more subtle. Importantly, for the purposes of this webpage, within individual galaxies the metal content is not homogeneous. This makes qualitative sense - different parts of the galaxy are not the same. For example, galaxies have been seen to evolve "inside out", meaning that the central part of the galaxy evolved prior to the regions exterior.
